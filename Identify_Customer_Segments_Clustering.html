Discussion 2.1: Apply Feature Scaling¶

I scaled features using StandardScaler, as suggested, which transforms variables into z-scores. The transformed variables have zero mean and unit variance. Scaling is necessary for principal components analysis.

Principal components analysis (PCA) produces the best rank-k approximation of the original variance matrix (the variance matrix of our dataframe in its scaled feature space). Using a singular value decomposition, PCA minimzes the squared norm of the difference between that pre-PCA variance matrix and the variance matrix of the principal components (i.e. the left singular vectors scaled by their corresponding singular values). In other words, PCA minimizes the difference in total variance.

When features are in different scales, the features in larger scales are likely to have greater variance and the PCA process will therefore weight them higher when minimizing the difference total variance. Variance scales quadratically with unit size (i.e. Var(cX) = ccVar(X) where c is a constant). In our case, without scaling the data the first principal component appears to explain more than 99% of the variance--a nonsensical result that I accidently produced with a coding error. But feature scales are arbitrary--how we measure information is always chosen by humans--so we are justified in transforming variables into units that better capture the relationships we wish to estimate. Standardizing our features puts them in the same space, giving them equal weight in the PCA process.

I want to briefly discuss missing data. I used mean imputation because sklearn hasn't yet implemented multiple imputation. Approaches like mean imputation can often increase bias. such methods are generally inappropriate for PCA because they artificially shrink the variances of features with imputed values. Still, mean imputation may less biased than listwise deletion in many cases. Sklearn is adapting to the needs of its users and will soon implement its sklearn.impute.IterativeImputer module, which is currently available in the developer version of sklearn. I intend to update this notebook when that module is available.

Discussion 2.2: Perform Dimensionality Reduction¶

I used principal components analysis (PCA) to find and keep the smallest number of principal components that explain at least 30% of the total feature variance while taking the "curse of dimensionality" in mind. The choice to ensure the transformed data matrix explains at least 30% of the feature variance is technically arbitrary. This decision rule led me to keep five principal components. This corresponds to a ~96% reduction in the column dimensionality of our data, from the 120 features of the pre-PCA design matrix to seven transformed features.

I aimed to keep a measure of dimensionality--the median distance between the origin and a point were our data uniformly distributed within a unit hypersphere--below 0.1. I found the median distance is ~0.06 with five features. The five best transformed features, where "best" is measured in explanatory power, explain ~30.03% of the total variance.

Using six components, we can explain 32.2% of the total variance with a median distance of ~0.098. It would be reasonble to keep as many as seven principal components. Keeping seven would be more marketable because we could claim to explain at least one third of the variance, but keeping five increases the likelihood of coherent and actionable clusters.

# takes a list of feature labels from a dataframe fitted to PCA, the fitted PCA object, and eigenvector index k
# returns list of label-weight tuples sorted by weight in descending order
# weights are singular value k * kth eigenvector
# i.e. returns weight of each feature associated with principal direction k
# assumes feature labels are in the same order as the data columns fitted to PCA
def map_pca_weights(labels, pca, k):
    sv = pca.singular_values_[k]
    v = pca.components_[k]
    fw_pairs = zip(labels, v*sv)
    sorted_fw_pairs = sorted(fw_pairs, key=lambda x: abs(x[1]), reverse=True)
    return list(sorted_fw_pairs)

def plot_pca_weight_map(fw_pairs):
    # plot feature-weight pairs
    features, weights = zip(*fw_pairs)
    plt.bar(range(len(features)), weights, width=0.7, zorder=2)
    plt.xticks(range(len(features)), features, rotation=40)
    plt.grid(axis='y', linestyle='-.', zorder=1)
    plt.title('Weight of kth Principal Component in Direction of Each Feature')
    plt.ylabel('Weight', rotation='horizontal', labelpad=30)
    plt.xlabel('Feature')
    plt.show()

Principal components represent latent features underlying the observed features. The principal component vectors can be interpreted (somewhat subjectively).

The first three prinicpal components I identified each cluster together related attributes.

The second vector describes relationships between financial reservedness, personal reservedness,conscientiousness, and age. The variable may capture elements of risk-aversion and conscientiousness, and possibly how those attributes change over a person's lifespan.

In summary, the principal components have meaning as latent variables which connect underlying relationships between the original features to form new ways of describing observations. In the present case, the transformed features have clear internal consistency. However, interpretations latent variables are subject to human biases.

Discussion 3.1: Apply Clustering to General Population¶

I clustered the population into eleven segments. I made this decision based on the marginal improvement in within-cluster variance, the Silhouette Coefficient, and the Calinski-Harabaz Score.

The Silhouette Coefficient is a scaled difference of the distance between clusters and the distance of points to their cluster means. It performs well with convex clusters, and is higher when clusters are dense and well-separated. The coefficient range is between -1 and 1, with higher scores representing "better" clustering.

The Calinski-Harabaz Score is the ratio of mean between-cluster variance and mean within-cluster variance. A well-defined cluster model has high variance (i.e. dispersion) between clusters and low within-cluster variance, so a higher Calinski-Harabaz Score suggests a better solution.

I chose to implement elven clusters because this value produces a maximum for the Silhouette Coefficient and is intuitively appropriate given the mean square error "elbow chart". I have tried several adjustments to my preprocessing pipeline, large and small, and have been unable to produce substantively better results.

If we think the company's customer base to be universal, then the cluster assignment proportions should be fairly similar between the two. If there are only particular segments of the population that are interested in the company's products, then we should see a mismatch from one to the other. If there is a higher proportion of persons in a cluster for the customer data compared to the general population (e.g. 5% of persons are assigned to a cluster for the general population, but 15% of the customer data is closest to that cluster's centroid) then that suggests the people in that cluster to be a target audience for the company. On the other hand, the proportion of the data in a cluster being larger in the general population than the customer data (e.g. only 2% of customers closest to a population centroid that captures 6% of the data) suggests that group of persons to be outside of the target demographics.

Discussion 3.3: Compare Customer Data to Demographics Data¶

The company's customer base is highly overrepresented in the second and 11th clusters, and underrepresented in the remaining clusters. I compared the first three principal component distributions for clusters two and 11.

The first principal component describes relationships between family size, community size, geographical mobility, and socioeconomic status. The associated latent variable may capture elements of rural vs. urban community, community cohesion, and community wealth. Higher values are related to larger families in larger communities, with lower socioeconomic status but higher overall net income.

The second principal component describes relationships between financial reservedness, personal reservedness,conscientiousness, and age. The variable may capture elements of risk-aversion and conscientiousness, and possibly how those attributes change over a person's lifespan. Higher values are related to older people who are less risk-averse and traditional and more financially responsible.

The third principal component describes personality traits associated with extraversion, prosicial behavior, antisocial behavior, and gender. Higher values are related to lower extraversion and social behavior, and higher disagreeability.

Compared to other clusters, the second cluster has much lower and more concentrated values in the first principal and third components. Cluster two has a narrower second component distribution than the combined other clusters, but the distribution has a similar median.

Compared to other clusters, the 11th cluster has much lower values for the first principal component, slightly higher values for the second principal component, and much higher values for the third component. These three distributions are narrow (i.e. have low variance).

Interestingly, each of the three prinicipal components I examined have bimodal distributions. This suggests they may be better represented by two distinct variables. We may be able to improve our model by collecting more data. By increasing our sample size, we can create coherent clusters in a higher dimensional feature space.

About 60% of the company's customer base are concentrated in the second and 11th clusters, while only 15% of the broader German demography are assigned to those clusters.
